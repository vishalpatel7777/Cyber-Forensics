== Executive Summary

[quote, "Federal Digital Forensics Expert (18 years)"]
____
A single mistake in handling digital evidence can make or break an entire investigation. Chain of Custody is not paperwork — it is the backbone of admissible evidence.
____

The **Chain of Custody (CoC)** is the chronological, documented trail that validates the integrity of digital evidence from the moment of collection to its presentation in court.  
A broken chain compromises admissibility, enabling defense attorneys to challenge authenticity and potentially dismantle even the strongest case.

Proper CoC requires meticulous recording of *who*, *what*, *when*, *where*, and *why* evidence was accessed or transferred.  
This document outlines the full workflow, modern practices (2025), case studies, and best-practice recommendations.

'''

== Part I: Concept & Criticality of Chain of Custody

=== 1. Definition

Chain of Custody is:

[example,title="Formal Definition"]
====
The documented, chronological process of collecting, handling, preserving, transferring, and securing digital evidence while ensuring its authenticity and integrity are legally defensible.
====

A valid CoC document records:

* Who collected or accessed the evidence  
* Where it was stored  
* Why access occurred  
* When transfers or actions occurred  
* How evidence was transported, duplicated, or stored  

=== 2. Why CoC Matters (Modern Legal Perspective)

* Ensures evidence is **credible**, **authentic**, and **untampered**  
* Establishes **trust** in entire investigative process  
* Prevents legal challenges and evidence dismissal  
* Ensures admissibility in court  
* Protects investigators from allegations of misconduct  

[WARNING]
====
If CoC is incomplete or missing, evidence can be ruled inadmissible — even if it is technically accurate.
====

'''

== Part II: The Four Pillars of Maintaining CoC

[cols="1,3,3",options="header"]
|===
| Step | Description | Key Goals

| 1. Evidence Collection  
| Identify, secure, and document potential digital evidence. Use **write blockers**, photograph devices, record serial numbers.  
| Preserve original state; avoid unintentional modification.

| 2. Documentation & Labeling  
| Assign unique IDs, record date/time/location, list personnel involved. Missing documentation = legal risk.  
| Establish complete evidence traceability.

| 3. Secure Storage & Access Control  
| Store evidence in a controlled-access forensic lab. Maintain access logs.  
| Prevent unauthorized access or tampering.

| 4. Transfer & Handling  
| Every movement requires logs, signatures, sealed bags, and CoC forms.  
| Preserve authenticity during transport.
|===

'''

== Part III: Detailed Workflow Breakdown

=== Step 1: Evidence Collection

. Secure the physical or digital scene  
. Photograph devices before touching  
. Document serial numbers, model numbers, and visible identifiers  
. Use **hardware write blockers** for disk imaging  
. Extract volatile data first when applicable (memory, sessions)  
. NEVER power off a system unnecessarily  

[CAUTION]
====
Unplugging or shutting down a machine may destroy volatile evidence (RAM), altering timestamps or processes.
====

---

=== Step 2: Documentation & Labeling

* Assign **unique case identifiers**  
* Record:  
**Date**, **Time**, **Location**, **Collector Name**, **Collector Role**  
* Label every item (device, disk, USB, cloud export)  
* Cross-reference logs with photographs and serial numbers  

[IMPORTANT]
====
Missing ANY detail allows defense attorneys to argue that evidence integrity was compromised.
====

---

=== Step 3: Secure Storage & Access Control

Evidence must be held in a **forensic lab** with:

* Tamper-evident packaging  
* Controlled access  
* CCTV or audit logs  
* Fireproof and humidity-controlled vaults  

Every access is logged with:

* Who accessed  
* When they accessed  
* Why access was needed  
* What they did  

[sidebar,title="Modern Note (2025)"]
****
Digital storage now commonly includes **cloud evidence vaults** with automated logging, immutable storage (WORM), and blockchain-backed audit trails.
****

---

=== Step 4: Transfer & Handling

Whenever evidence moves:

* Document the transfer  
* Include CoC forms  
* Use tamper-proof bags  
* Record seal numbers  
* Obtain signatures from both sender and receiver  

[example,title="Sample Transfer Log Entry"]
====
Item: MacBook Pro (S/N XCV-88932)  
From: Officer #12 (Person A)  
To: Forensics Lab Technician #08 (Person B)  
Purpose: Imaging & volatile extraction  
Sealed Bag #: 553-22-M  
Timestamp: 2025-11-22 14:33  
====

'''

== Part V: Best Practices for Evidence Integrity (2025 Standards)

=== 1. Use Forensic Tools

* Write blockers (Tableau, WiebeTech)  
* Imaging tools:  
**FTK Imager, EnCase, Autopsy, Guymager, Velociraptor, SIFT Workstation**  
* Memory tools:  
**Volatility 3, winpmem, AVML**  

=== 2. Maintain Access Logs Rigorously

Log everything:

* Who touched it  
* Why  
* For how long  
* What actions they performed  

=== 3. Timestamp Every Action

Use **synchronized UTC time sources** across departments.  
Time drift = legal vulnerability.

=== 4. Verify Data Integrity (Hashing)

Use cryptographic fingerprinting:

[stem]
++++
Hash = SHA256(data)
++++

Techniques:

* SHA-256 (recommended)  
* SHA-512  
* MD5 (only for legacy comparison)  

Re-hash at every transfer to validate integrity.

=== 5. Team Training & Legal Awareness

* Regular training sessions  
* Scenario-based practice  
* Understanding legal implications  
* Awareness of international data handling laws (GDPR, IT Act, etc.)

'''
